TL;DR — EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

🖼️
4-up impact stat grid — large numbers

The wealth management industry is at an inflection point. Relationship Managers (RMs) are drowning in admin work, juggling 5+ disconnected systems, and burning hours assembling client reports. Clients want goal-oriented advice. Compliance wants systemic visibility.

We rebuilt the RM workspace around three principles: 1. Centralize the household — one client, one view, all accounts 2. Shift from reactive to proactive — surface risk before it surfaces itself 3. Offload cognition to AI — let humans do the human part

Headline Outcomes

  • Pre-meeting prep: 2 hours → 30 seconds
  • Drift detection: reactive (post-breach) → predictive (7 days out)
  • RM time freed: ~30% of week reclaimed for client-facing work
  • Client Satisfaction (NPS): 32 → 64 (Doubled)

    UX STRATEGY MINDSET

    🖼️
    A diagram showing your design philosophy — e.g., 3 concentric circles: Human → Workflow → Data

    How I Approached This Problem

    Most wealth platforms are built database-first—they dump 40 columns of raw data into a table and ask the Relationship Manager to hunt for the insight. I flipped this paradigm: I designed workflow-first. Every screen in Strand Finance was built to answer a specific temporal question: What needs my attention at 9:00 AM? What context do I need at 1:30 PM before a call? Every interface had to answer these questions in under 5 seconds.

    Three Guiding Principles

    1. Workflow before data. Every screen earns its place by serving a moment in the RM's day. 2. AI augments, never replaces. AI handles the synthesis; the RM owns the relationship. 3. Trust through transparency. Every number shows its source, its timestamp, and its confidence.

    THE CONTEXT — WHY NOW?

    🖼️
    Industry forces visual — 4 cards or a force-field diagram

    Before designing a single pixel, I mapped the macroeconomic forces reshaping the industry. Understanding why the industry is moving was the unlock for what to design.

    1. Fee Compression

    Robo-advisors charging 0.25% are squeezing traditional 1% firms. To survive, firms need tooling that lets RMs serve more clients without sacrificing quality.

    2. The Great Wealth Transfer

    ~$84T will pass from Boomers to Gen X/Millennials over the next 20 years. The next generation expects seamless digital UX. Firms that don't modernize lose those assets.

    3. Aging RM Workforce

    The average advisor is 55+. Firms need intuitive tools so younger RMs can ramp up fast.

    4. Explosion of Alternative Assets

    Private equity, crypto, direct real estate — increasingly part of HNWI portfolios, but the data is unstructured chaos.
    🚀 AI Opportunity: Models can ingest unstructured capital call PDFs and normalize them into the dashboard instantly.

    HYPOTHESIS

    🖼️
    Hypothesis card — large typography, framed like a thesis statement

    Core Hypothesis

    If we centralize the household, shift from reactive to proactive monitoring, and deeply integrate AI into the synthesis layer,
    then RMs will reclaim cognitive bandwidth for relationship-building,
    as measured by reduced pre-meeting prep time, increased client-facing hours, and earlier risk detection.

    Sub-Hypotheses

    1. H1 — A household-first IA will reduce data-gathering time by ≥70% 2. H2 — AI-drafted narratives will be accepted with ≤20% edits by RMs in their own voice 3. H3 — Predictive drift detection will reduce compliance escalations by ≥50% 4. H4 — A mobile companion will increase same-day note logging from 12% to 75%

    SERVICE BLUEPRINT

    🖼️
    Service blueprint — frontstage / backstage / systems swimlanes

    Layers Mapped

  • Customer Actions (Client + RM)
  • Frontstage (UI surfaces the RM/Client touches)
  • Backstage (RM internal workflows, ops team)
  • Support Systems (Custodian, Orion, Salesforce, SharePoint, Compliance engine)
  • AI Layer (new) — entity resolution, NLG, predictive drift, NLP

    • The blueprint revealed a massive structural flaw: the backend support systems (Salesforce, Orion) were acting purely as raw data silos. The entire cognitive burden of aggregating, formatting, and analyzing that data was pushed onto the 'Frontstage'—meaning the RM had to do it manually. We realized the AI layer had to sit exactly between the backend and the UI to act as an automatic synthesis engine.

    REFLECTION & LEARNINGS

    🖼️
    A personal note — maybe a photo of the team or a handwritten note from a user

    What I'd Do Differently

  • "I underestimated how much resistance there would be to AI-drafted narratives. RMs needed a 'sounds like me' feedback loop before they'd trust it. I'd build that earlier."
  • "The mobile companion launched 6 weeks late because we tried to feature-match the desktop. Mobile should have been scoped as 'high-level metrics only' from day one."

    • What I Learned About AI in UX

    1. AI is a material, not a feature. Treating it like a "ChatGPT button" failed. Threading it through the workflow worked. 2. Confidence is a UI element. Showing how sure the AI is mattered more than the answer itself. 3. The hard part isn't the model — it's the moment. Designing when AI shows up was harder than designing what it says.

    What Surprised Me

    The senior RMs (aged 55+) adopted the AI features significantly faster than the junior RMs. The juniors were skeptical of the tech, but the seniors had lived with the pain of manual reporting for so long that they immediately recognized the massive value of automated synthesis.
